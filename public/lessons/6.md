### Theory

You have been working with git entirely on your own machine. But git is designed for collaboration, and that means understanding the difference between what lives on your computer and what lives somewhere else.

**Your local repository**

Everything you have done so far — `git init`, `git add`, `git commit` — happens locally. The commits are stored in a hidden `.git` folder on your hard drive. No one else can see them. If your computer broke today, that history would be gone.
===
**The remote repository**

A remote is a copy of your repository stored on another server, usually somewhere like GitHub. It acts as a shared hub. You push your commits up to it, and anyone you are collaborating with can pull them down.

The remote does not update automatically. Your local and remote repositories are two separate things that you keep in sync manually using `git push` and `git pull`.
===
**Tracking branches**

When you push a branch, git creates a remote-tracking reference like `origin/main`. This is your local copy's last known state of the remote. You can see both in the Timeline panel: `main` is where your local commits are, and `origin/main` is where the remote is.

They move apart as you make new commits locally or as others push to the remote. `git push` moves `origin/main` forward to match your `main`. `git pull` moves your `main` forward to match `origin/main`.
===
**`git log`**

`git log` shows you the commit history of your current branch. Each entry shows the commit hash, author, date, and message. It is a useful way to see what has happened in your local repository.

### Further reading

**What is `origin`?**

`origin` is just a name. It is the conventional default name for your main remote, but you could name it anything. When you ran `git remote add origin <url>` in lesson 5, you were saying: "call this remote `origin` and point it at this URL."

You can have more than one remote. Some teams have an `origin` pointing to their own fork and an `upstream` pointing to the main project repository.

**What happens if you never push?**

Your local commits are safe as long as your machine is safe. But if you want others to see your work, or if you want a backup that does not depend on your own hardware, you need to push. Teams typically push regularly so work is never siloed on one person's laptop.

**`git log` flags**

`git log` has many useful options. `git log --oneline` shows a compact one-line summary per commit. `git log --graph` draws a text-based picture of the branch history. These are helpful as projects grow and commit histories become longer.

### Further reading button
Label: Why are local and remote kept separate?

Keeping local and remote separate is a deliberate design choice in git, and a powerful one.

You can work entirely offline, making commits freely, without needing an internet connection or a server. Your full history is always available on your machine.

When you are ready to share, you push. This means you can clean up your local commits, write better messages, or squash small changes together before anyone else sees them.

Contrast this with older systems like SVN where every commit went straight to a central server. Git's local-first model gives you much more control and makes working offline, on planes, or in environments without reliable internet completely practical.
